# Upstream Decarbonization Investments for Hyperscale Data Centers

*A data center construction site utilizing large amounts of concrete and steel. Decarbonizing such materials is crucial to reducing the embodied (Scope 3) emissions of hyperscale infrastructure.*

## Introduction

Hyperscale data center companies (e.g. Google, Microsoft, Meta, Apple) are expanding rapidly, driving significant demand for **steel, cement, aluminum, copper, and rare earth materials** in construction and hardware supply chains. The production of these materials is highly carbon-intensive - for example, steel and cement each account for roughly **7-8% of global CO₂ emissions** - making them *hard-to-abate* sectors critical to climate goals. As these tech giants push toward ambitious climate targets (many aim for net-zero emissions across their value chains by 2030), tackling upstream **Scope 3** emissions from **materials and construction** has become a strategic imperative. Traditional approaches like purchasing carbon offsets or asking suppliers to change are proving insufficient. Instead, **direct investment in emerging "green" technologies** for producing low-carbon materials offers a proactive path to slash embodied emissions at the source.

This report presents a prioritized, investment-grade list of **green technologies and companies** (from early-stage to near-commercial) that can decarbonize the key materials in data center infrastructure. We focus on the hardest-to-abate supply chain inputs - **green steel, low-carbon cement, sustainable aluminum, cleaner copper refining, and rare-earth magnets** - with an emphasis on North American opportunities (while noting global developments for context). For each category, we identify the priority technologies, leading innovators or consortia, the capital investment gap, and the potential environmental impact relative to business-as-usual. We then map these opportunities to the stated sustainability goals of hyperscalers, illustrating how **direct capital deployment upstream** could accelerate corporate climate targets and yield measurable impact. In closing, we synthesize the findings into a compelling lecture-style narrative (suitable for a 30-minute presentation) covering historical precedents, geopolitical context, counterarguments, urgency, and recommendations - emphasizing the consequences of inaction versus the transformative potential of bold investment now.

## Priority Upstream Decarbonization Sectors & Technologies

Achieving deep decarbonization of data center supply chains requires targeting the **materials that drive embodied emissions** in construction and hardware. Below we outline five priority sectors - **Steel, Cement/Concrete, Aluminum, Copper, and Rare Earth Magnets** - highlighting key low-carbon technologies, leading companies, investment needs, and the potential climate impact of scaling these solutions.

### Green Steel

* **Why It's Priority:** Steel is ubiquitous in data center structures (frames, rebar, server racks) and manufacturing equipment. Producing steel is **emissions-intensive (\~7% of global CO₂)**, primarily due to coal-fired blast furnaces. Decarbonizing steel is essential to bend the emissions curve of infrastructure construction.
* **Priority Technologies:** *Green steel* initiatives focus on replacing coal with cleaner energy and processes. Two main pathways have emerged: **Hydrogen Direct Reduction (H₂-DRI)**, which uses green hydrogen to reduce iron ore (paired with electric arc furnaces), and **Electrolysis-based steelmaking**, which uses electricity (ideally renewable) to directly produce iron metal. Carbon capture on blast furnaces is another option, but less transformative. Key technologies include **100% hydrogen DRI** (which emits water vapor instead of CO₂) and **molten oxide electrolysis** (producing iron via electrochemistry with oxygen as the byproduct).
* **Leading Companies/Consortia:**  Early leaders span both startups and industry alliances. In the U.S., **Boston Metal** (an MIT spinout) is scaling a *molten oxide electrolysis* process to make steel with zero direct CO₂; it raised over \$262 million in 2023 to pilot this at scale. **Electra** is another U.S. startup developing low-temperature electrochemical ironmaking. In Europe, **H₂ Green Steel** (Sweden) and the HYBRIT consortium (SSAB, LKAB, Vattenfall in Sweden) are building the first commercial hydrogen-based steel plants, aiming to produce fossil-free steel by 2025-2030. Major steelmakers are also shifting: e.g. ArcelorMittal is retrofitting plants for DRI-EAF, and a coalition of companies and buyers formed the **Sustainable Steel Buyers Platform** to secure near-zero emissions steel in North America. Notably, **Microsoft** and others have joined this platform to catalyze the first **2 million tons** of green steel production by the late 2020s.
* **Capital Investment Gap:** Decarbonizing steel globally requires massive investment in new plants and clean energy infrastructure. Even maintaining expected steel demand with current methods needs \~\$30 billion in capital *per year*; achieving net-zero steel adds about **\$6 billion per year extra** for low-CO₂ technologies. That's roughly a 20% increase (\$180 billion cumulative through 2050). In addition, an estimated **\$2 trillion** is needed to build out supporting infrastructure (renewable electricity for electrification and hydrogen, plus carbon capture storage). This represents a major funding gap, especially for first-of-a-kind green steel plants that face a **"green premium" of up to 50-60% higher operating costs** in the near term. Bridging this gap will require both policy support and value-chain investment.
* **Impact Potential:** The climate payoff is enormous. Replacing coal-based steel with near-zero carbon steel can cut emissions per ton by **85-95%**. Early action this decade is critical: analysis suggests that deploying green steel at scale by 2030 could eliminate **1.3 gigatons of CO₂/year by 2030** (a 37% reduction vs 2020), keeping a 1.5 °C pathway alive. By 2050, a fully decarbonized steel sector avoids **35 gigatons CO₂ cumulatively** - a huge wedge in the remaining carbon budget. Every data center that uses green steel framing instead of conventional steel brings a tangible emissions drop versus business-as-usual. In addition, investing in scrap recycling and **electric arc furnaces** (which can run on 100% clean power) provides immediate emissions reductions as electricity grids decarbonize. Green steel is thus a cornerstone: without it, steel could become the **single largest industrial emitter** as other sectors clean up.

### Low-Carbon Cement & Concrete

* **Why It's Priority:** Cement is the "glue" in concrete, used extensively in data center foundations, walls, and cooling structures. Unfortunately, cement production is one of the hardest processes to decarbonize, emitting CO₂ both from fossil fuel heat and the chemistry of limestone calcination. Cement and concrete contribute roughly **8% of global CO₂**. Reducing the *embodied carbon* of concrete is vital for sustainable construction.
* **Priority Technologies:** There is no single silver bullet - a suite of innovations is emerging. These include **clinker substitution** (using lower-carbon binders like fly ash, slag, or calcined clay to replace some Portland cement), **novel chemistries** for cement that emit less CO₂, **carbon capture** on cement kilns, and **carbon curing** (injecting CO₂ into concrete during mixing or curing to mineralize it). For example, **CarbonCure** and **CarbonBuilt** inject captured CO₂ into concrete mixes, reducing cement needs and permanently storing CO₂. **CarbiCrete** replaces cement entirely with steel slag and cures concrete with CO₂, making a *carbon-negative* block. Another startup, **Brimstone**, is developing cement made from calcium silicate rock that releases no CO₂ in production. Importantly, **design optimization** (using less concrete where possible or switching to alternatives like timber for structure) also cuts demand.
* **Leading Companies/Consortia:** Several North American innovators are at the forefront. **CarbonCure** (Canada) is already deploying CO₂ injection systems in concrete plants worldwide, and **CarbonBuilt** (U.S.) won the Carbon XPRIZE for its low-carbon concrete curing process. **Prometheus Materials** (U.S.) uses bio-based processes (algae-grown limestone) for a carbon-neutral cement alternative - it attracted investment from Microsoft's Climate Innovation Fund. Major cement companies (LafargeHolcim, Heidelberg, etc.) have pilot projects for carbon capture and low-clinker cements. In 2023, **Meta (Facebook)** announced agreements to *invest in scaling CarbonBuilt's technology*, upgrading concrete plants to use its CO₂-storing binder - which can **cut concrete's carbon footprint by up to 70%**. Meta is also financing CarbiCrete's scale-up as part of its supplier decarbonization strategy. These efforts, along with industry consortia (e.g. the Global Cement and Concrete Association's "Net Zero 2050" roadmap), aim to commercialize low-carbon concrete formulas for widespread use.
* **Capital Investment Gap:** Transforming the cement/concrete industry globally will require on the order of **\$1-1.5 trillion** by 2050. A recent in-depth analysis found that achieving net-zero concrete by 2050 would require **\$1.42 trillion** in cumulative investment (in production upgrades, carbon capture, and supportive infrastructure) - a **35% increase** over business-as-usual spending. If optimized use and material efficiency are maximized, in-sector investment might even drop slightly (as less capacity is needed), but huge capital is still needed for carbon capture (\~\$390 billion for CCUS on plants) and clean energy for kiln heat. In North America, the funding gap is being partly addressed by new government support (the U.S. DOE's recent \$6 billion program for industrial decarbonization includes grants for low-carbon cement plants). Still, private investment is needed to build first commercial-scale *clean cement* plants and to retrofit existing facilities. Currently, low-carbon cements often carry a cost premium (cement costs could rise \~40-120% with carbon capture, equating to a 1.5-3% increase in overall construction cost). Bridging this premium through innovation and investment will be key to scaling.
* **Impact Potential:** Without intervention, cement/concrete is on track to emit a staggering **98 gigatons of CO₂ from 2022-2050**, roughly **double** the allowable emissions if we are to stay on a 1.5 °C path. Implementing known decarbonization levers could reduce that dramatically - for instance, simply optimizing how concrete is used can cut demand and avoid a large chunk of emissions early. By 2050, a portfolio of solutions (efficiency, alternative materials, CCUS) can bring the sector to net-zero. Each approach contributes: *Supplementary cementitious materials* (like fly ash or slag) can cut cement clinker usage by 20-50%; *new binders and chemistries* could eliminate process emissions; *CCUS* could capture 50%+ of remaining emissions. Even today, switching to available low-carbon concrete mixes can yield **30-50% reductions** in embodied carbon for projects. For hyperscale companies building data centers, using these materials at scale (and investing to expand their supply) could reduce the embodied carbon of new facilities by over **30-60%**, as Microsoft demonstrated with a recent pilot using low-carbon concrete and even timber. In sum, decarbonizing cement is challenging but pivotal - it offers one of the largest single-sector climate wins if successful, given the sheer volume of concrete used globally.

### Sustainable Aluminum

* **Why It's Priority:** Aluminum is used in data center equipment (server casings, electrical components, cooling infrastructure) and sometimes building facades or structural elements. Aluminum production is electricity-intensive and traditionally involves carbon anodes that emit CO₂. While aluminum's share of global emissions (\~2-3%) is smaller than steel or cement, it is significant and growing with demand for electronics and vehicles. Crucially, **clean aluminum** can help hyperscalers lower the footprint of servers and devices (Apple's products, for instance, use a lot of aluminum).
* **Priority Technologies:** The two keys to decarbonizing aluminum are **clean electricity** (since \~60% of aluminum's CO₂ comes from power, using renewable energy for smelting can cut most of it) and **inert anode technology** (to eliminate the direct CO₂ from the smelting reaction). Conventional smelting (Hall-Héroult process) uses carbon anodes that burn away, releasing CO₂. New inert anodes (made of ceramics or alloys) allow aluminum oxide to be reduced *emitting pure oxygen instead of CO₂*. The flagship effort here is **Elysis**, a joint venture developing inert anode cells. Increased **recycling** is another huge lever - recycling aluminum uses \~5% of the energy of primary production. Thus, technologies to improve scrap collection and re-melting capacity are also critical (though for data centers, scrap comes more from end-of-life devices/racks).
* **Leading Companies/Consortia:** **Elysis** (a JV of Alcoa and Rio Tinto, launched with support from Apple and the Canadian government) is leading inert anode commercialization. In 2019, Apple purchased the **first batch of carbon-free aluminum** from Elysis, produced at a pilot facility in Quebec. This aluminum, made with a ceramic anode, emits oxygen gas instead of CO₂. Elysis is scaling up for larger production by 2024. Apple's involvement (including direct investment and off-take commitment) is a notable example of a downstream company catalyzing an upstream green technology. Other major aluminum players are also moving: *Hydro* (Norway) produces low-carbon aluminum using hydropower; Rusal has tested inert anodes as well. In the US, *Alcoa* has a pilot inert anode smelter in Pittsburgh. **Electrification of heat** in alumina refining (the step before smelting) is another area with startups like **Rusal's ALLOW** technology. Additionally, consortia like the **First Movers Coalition** include companies pledging to buy low-carbon aluminum for their products, helping create market demand.
* **Capital Investment Gap:** Fully decarbonizing the aluminum sector globally could require on the order of **\$500 billion to \$1.5 trillion** in cumulative investment. Much of this is for clean power capacity (since making enough renewable electricity for all smelters is a heavy lift), as well as retrofitting smelters with new technology. Inert anode cells will require retooling or new facilities - Elysis, for example, needed an initial \~\$150 million for R\&D and pilot lines. Widespread deployment will demand several large demonstration plants in the 2020s. There is a gap in financing these first units, as traditional aluminum producers are cost-sensitive in a commodity market. However, some costs are coming down: Elysis expects its process to have *lower operating costs* long-term than traditional methods (since it avoids the cost of carbon anodes and potentially runs more efficiently). Government incentives (like U.S. production tax credits for industrial decarbonization in the Inflation Reduction Act) can help close the economic gap. Still, targeted corporate investment or offtake agreements may be needed to underwrite the initial premium for "green aluminum."
* **Impact Potential:** The environmental benefit of sustainable aluminum comes from two main reductions: cutting electricity emissions and eliminating process CO₂. Using 100% renewable electricity (hydro, solar, wind) for smelting can reduce aluminum's CO₂ footprint by \~75% (since most smelters today use coal or gas power). Inert anode technology can *eliminate the remaining direct emissions*, achieving potentially **zero direct CO₂**. Combined, these measures enable **carbon-free aluminum** at scale, avoiding the \~*16 tons CO₂* per ton that a coal-powered smelter emits. If the aluminum sector's decarbonization succeeds, it could avoid up to **0.5-1 Gt CO₂ per year by mid-century**, considering rising demand. While aluminum is not as large an emitter as steel or cement, its role is still significant, especially in a clean energy economy (for example, lightweight aluminum is used in EVs, solar frames, etc.). From a hyperscaler perspective, using *low-carbon aluminum* in servers and facility construction can help meet Scope 3 targets. Apple, by switching to recycled and Elysis aluminum for some products, has already cut millions of tons of CO₂. Similarly, if data center operators procure green aluminum for equipment casings, cable trays, or other components, they directly reduce product emissions. In sum, although the capital requirements are high, aluminum's decarbonization yields a comparatively straightforward emissions drop (thanks to the primacy of clean electricity) and is a necessary piece of the net-zero puzzle.

### Cleaner Copper Refining

* **Why It's Priority:** Copper is the lifeblood of electrical infrastructure - data centers contain vast quantities of copper in power cables, bus bars, circuit boards, and cooling systems. Copper's carbon footprint per ton is lower than steel or aluminum, but it still involves carbon-intensive steps (mining, grinding, smelting) and significant electricity use. Moreover, global copper demand is set to double by 2035 (driven by electrification), which could increase associated emissions. Decarbonizing copper refining ensures that the wiring and electronics enabling cloud computing are as green as the renewable energy they carry.
* **Priority Technologies:** Copper production has two main routes: **pyrometallurgical smelting** (for sulfide ores, dominant method) and **hydrometallurgical leaching** (for oxide ores or low-grade material). Smelting involves furnaces that often burn fossil fuels and emit SO₂ and CO₂. Key decarbonization levers include: electrifying the smelting process (e.g. using electric furnaces or novel electrolysis of copper sulfide concentrates) and expanding **catalytic leaching** technologies that can extract copper from low-grade ores at ambient conditions. By leaching instead of smelting, one can avoid the high-temperature, carbon-heavy steps. Another lever is **recycling**: copper is highly recyclable with much lower energy, so improving recycling technologies and recovery from e-waste reduces the need for new smelting.
* **Leading Companies/Consortia:** An exciting development in this space is **Jetti Resources** (U.S.), which has a breakthrough catalytic leaching process enabling extraction of copper from chalcopyrite (the most common copper ore) that traditional methods leave behind. Jetti's tech allows miners to produce copper from waste rock via leaching, drastically reducing the need for additional smelting. According to Jetti, this process *uses less energy and can cut CO₂ emissions by \~40%* per unit of copper. It's been piloted successfully, even **doubling production at a mine in Arizona** by liberating copper from leftover ore. Jetti has drawn investments from major mining companies and even downstream users like BMW. Another approach is direct electrolysis: researchers (e.g. Boston Metal's sister venture **Boston Electrometallurgical**) are investigating electrochemical methods to turn copper sulfide concentrate into copper metal in one step, powered by clean electricity. Large firms like Rio Tinto are also developing their own leaching and waste recovery technologies (e.g. Rio's "Nuton" technology). On the recycling front, companies such as **Aurubis** and **Redwood Materials** are scaling up recovery of copper from electronic scrap and lithium-ion batteries. While copper refining has not seen as many flashy startups as steel or cement, these innovations could quietly revolutionize copper supply with much lower emissions.
* **Capital Investment Gap:** The copper industry is mature, so changes can be slow and capital-intensive. Retrofitting smelters with new electric furnaces or building electrolysis cells will require substantial investment. One estimate suggests the sector needs on the order of **\$100+ billion** globally to decarbonize (including new mines, processes, and power sources), though precise figures are less published. However, specific investment gaps can be identified: for instance, Jetti's expansion to dozens of mines will require partnership funding to retrofit mine leach circuits. Each  large copper mine adopting catalytic leaching might need tens of millions in new equipment and site modifications - meaning *billions in aggregate* to roll out worldwide. The **BMW-led funding into Jetti** and others is a sign of downstream industries stepping up; more such direct investments or offtake agreements (e.g. automakers or tech firms securing low-carbon copper supply) could de-risk the scaling. Additionally, **renewable energy investments** are needed at smelter locations - many copper smelters (in Chile, China) currently rely on fossil electricity or on-site combustion. Funding solar and wind farms dedicated to powering copper refining is another aspect (the S\&P Global analysis noted \$500 billion+ needed to decarbonize energy supply for metals like aluminum and copper). All told, while the *incremental cost per ton* of low-carbon copper may be modest (\~<10% premium, especially if energy is renewable), the up-front capital to retool processes is substantial and often not allocated in miners' budgets. This is where hyperscalers' capital can potentially make a difference by accelerating tech demonstration in exchange for future green copper supply.
* **Impact Potential:** Cleaner copper refining may not grab headlines, but it has meaningful environmental benefits and supply security advantages. A 40% emissions cut via leaching (as Jetti achieves) means that for every tonne of copper (which normally emits \~3-5 tCO₂ in production), \~1-2 tCO₂ could be saved. Scale that to the expected \~50 million tonnes annual copper demand by 2035, and that's up to **50-100 million tonnes CO₂ avoided per year** versus business-as-usual - equivalent to taking many coal plants offline. Beyond climate, there are **co-benefits**: modern leaching techniques avoid the local air pollution (SO₂, particulate) of smelters, improving environmental outcomes for communities. They also allow more complete extraction of existing mines, meaning *less mining* for the same copper - reducing land/water impacts. For hyperscalers, securing low-carbon copper ensures that the massive amount of wiring in their facilities doesn't quietly undermine Scope 3 targets. It also aligns with the broader electrification trend: as these companies finance renewable energy and battery storage for operations, supporting cleaner copper production closes the loop (since copper is a key ingredient in renewables and grids). In short, while copper's emissions are smaller in proportion, decarbonizing its supply chain is an essential piece of greening the digital infrastructure ecosystem.

### Rare Earth Magnets and Materials

* **Why It's Priority:** Rare earth elements like neodymium and dysprosium are critical for the **permanent magnets** used in high-efficiency motors and generators - from wind turbines and electric vehicles to the hard drives and cooling fans in data centers. While the *volume* of rare earths in data centers is not huge, their supply chain has significant environmental and geopolitical issues. Over 90% of rare earth magnet production is currently in China, often with high carbon intensity and serious pollution from mining and processing. Hyperscalers pushing for sustainable hardware cannot ignore the embedded impacts of electronics and backup generators that rely on these materials. Additionally, sourcing sustainable rare earths aligns with supply chain resilience goals.
* **Priority Technologies:** The main ways to make rare earth magnets more sustainable are: **recycling and circular recovery** of magnets, **cleaner processing** of rare earth oxides into metals, and possibly **substitution/innovation** (developing new magnet materials or motor designs that use less or no rare earth content). Recycling is especially promising - magnets from end-of-life electronics can be collected and reprocessed into new magnets, avoiding the need for new mining. This not only cuts emissions (mining and separating rare earths is energy-intensive) but also alleviates toxic waste issues. On the processing side, advances in **separation chemistry** and electrolysis can reduce the typically heavy chemical and thermal steps. For example, using solvent extraction with better reagents, or direct reduction of rare earth oxides with hydrogen, could lower environmental impact. Even where mining is needed, **improved mining practices** (like those at Mountain Pass in California) can drastically reduce harm: using renewable power for milling, dry tailings disposal to avoid wastewater, etc.
* **Leading Companies/Consortia:** In the U.S., **Noveon Magnetics** (formerly Urban Mining Co.) is a standout startup focusing on *magnet-to-magnet recycling*. Noveon operates a facility in Texas that takes in scrap neodymium magnets (from electronics or manufacturing waste) and produces new high-performance NdFeB magnets. Their process is highly efficient - it achieves **90% energy savings** compared to traditional magnet manufacturing and cuts environmental impact in half. In fact, Noveon's recycled magnets (branded "EcoFlux") use 20% less rare earth material and still meet performance specs. They recently raised \$75 million to scale up, indicating growing investor interest in local magnet supply. Another player, **MP Materials**, has reopened the Mountain Pass mine and is building a domestic processing chain for rare earths. MP claims its U.S. operations will be far *greener than Asian counterparts*, which have caused *"nightmarish pollution"* - thanks to robust environmental controls and renewable energy use. MP is constructing a facility to make NdFeB magnets in the U.S. by 2025, supported by a Department of Defense grant. On the global stage, companies like **Lynas** (Australia/Malaysia) are also improving rare earth processing outside China with higher environmental standards. Additionally, consortia such as the DOE's **Critical Materials Institute** are driving R\&D on both recycling and substitute materials (like iron-nitride magnets that could reduce dependence on rare earths).
* **Capital Investment Gap:** Building a diversified, clean rare earth supply chain essentially requires recreating an industry that migrated offshore decades ago. This means capital for everything: new mining operations or expansions, separation plants, alloy and magnet manufacturing facilities, and collection/recycling systems. MP Materials, for example, is investing hundreds of millions to establish U.S.-based separation and magnet production. Noveon, as a startup, will need additional manufacturing lines and perhaps partnerships with electronics OEMs to secure feedstock. The U.S. government has stepped in with some funding (e.g. \~\$50-100 million grants to rare earth projects), but private investment is needed to scale commercial capacity. One challenge is that, compared to steel or cement, the *market size* for rare earth magnets is smaller - on the order of \$10-15 billion/year globally. Thus, the absolute capital needed is lower, but the willingness of private sector to invest has been limited due to past volatility in rare earth prices. This is where hyperscalers could play a role: by investing (alone or via consortia) in rare earth recycling facilities or signing long-term purchase agreements, they can provide the market certainty to justify new capacity. The **investment gap in recycling** is particularly addressable - today <1% of rare earth magnets are recycled, largely for lack of optimized logistics and facilities. With relatively modest investment (tens of millions for collection programs and recycling plants), that rate could rise dramatically, reducing the need for new mining. Overall, while rare earth magnet decarbonization might not require the multi-billion-dollar plants that steel does, it still needs strategic capital to jumpstart a cleaner supply chain.
* **Impact Potential:** In climate terms, rare earth magnet production is a smaller emitter, but cleaning it up has outsized benefits for enabling clean energy and technology. Recycled magnets have nearly **10× lower energy demand** than magnets from mined material, which means significantly lower CO₂ emissions (especially if recycling uses renewable electricity). Additionally, each ton of rare earth oxide recovered from waste avoids the toxic byproducts of mining (which in places like China have led to contaminated soil and radioactive waste lakes). Environmentally, a domestic, regulated supply chain can eliminate the grievous pollution associated with rare earths - aligning with broader corporate sustainability goals beyond carbon. From a **hyperscaler perspective**, using recycled rare earths in devices and data center components can slightly reduce Scope 3 carbon and also demonstrate responsible sourcing (Apple, for instance, has pledged to use 100% recycled rare earths in its device magnets by 2025). On a macro scale, having sustainable rare earth supplies enables the growth of wind turbines and EVs that hyperscalers often invest in (through renewable energy PPAs or fleet electrification), thus indirectly supporting decarbonization of power and transport. Finally, there's a *geopolitical climate synergy*: reducing reliance on a single country's rare earth supply not only is good for business resilience, but also allows higher environmental oversight - ensuring that as the world deploys clean technologies, we aren't shifting the environmental burden to unregulated mines. In summary, while rare earths are a niche, investing to make their supply chain cleaner yields a triple win: climate-friendly manufacturing, reduced ecological damage, and secure access to materials essential for the clean energy transition and digital economy.

## Alignment with Hyperscalers' Sustainability Goals

The opportunities above closely map onto the sustainability and climate commitments of the major hyperscale data center operators. Each of these companies has set bold targets to address not only operational (Scope 1 and 2) emissions, but also **Scope 3 value chain emissions**, which include the embodied carbon of construction materials and hardware. Below we outline each company's goals and initiatives, and how upstream investments in green materials would accelerate their progress:

### Google (Alphabet)

**Climate Goals:** Google aims to achieve **net-zero emissions across all operations and value chain by 2030**. This includes a target to *halve* its total Scope 1-3 emissions by 2030 and then neutralize the remainder, likely through high-quality carbon removals. Google is also famous for its goal to run on **24/7 carbon-free energy by 2030** for its data centers, addressing Scope 2. On Scope 3 (which for Google includes construction of data centers, manufacturing of IT equipment, and supplier energy use), Google has started to move from offsets to direct action - for instance, it is engaging suppliers to adopt renewable energy and exploring lower-carbon materials in its building designs.

**Relevant Initiatives:** In 2023, Google joined the **Sustainable Steel Buyers Platform**, a buyers' coalition to procure near-zero emissions steel. Through this platform (led by RMI), Google and others send a strong demand signal to steel producers that low-carbon steel will have ready buyers in the tech sector, helping justify investment in green steel capacity. Google has also been an advocate of the **First Movers Coalition**, committing to purchase a percentage of its future materials (like steel and cement) from low-carbon sources by 2030. In its latest environmental report, Google notes its focus on **embodied carbon in data center construction** and is working with construction firms to pilot low-carbon concrete and recycled materials. For example, Google has used **supplementary cementitious materials** (like coal fly ash) in some data center concrete to reduce clinker content, and it continuously refines its building standards to cut material usage. Furthermore, Google's Circular Economy commitments (to **reuse or recycle 100% of servers** and reduce waste) complement the idea of using recycled metals (like aluminum and copper) in new equipment.

**Opportunity Alignment:** Directly investing in upstream decarbonization would strongly reinforce Google's Scope 3 goals. For instance, by funding a *green cement startup* or a *low-carbon steel mill*, Google could ensure a future supply of those materials for its data center builds, thus locking in emissions reductions. This approach goes beyond asking suppliers to change - it helps create the suppliers. It also aligns with Google's history of infrastructure investment: just as Google invested in renewable energy projects through long-term power purchase agreements to achieve zero-carbon electricity, it can invest in **"zero-carbon steel and concrete"** supply to achieve zero-carbon construction. These investments would make Google's 2030 net-zero target more attainable with less reliance on offsets. They also future-proof Google against emerging regulations like carbon border adjustments or embodied carbon limits in building codes. In short, Google's climate leadership in operations can extend to **value-chain leadership**, catalyzing the decarbonization of heavy industries that feed into its footprint.

### Microsoft

**Climate Goals:** Microsoft has one of the most aggressive corporate climate commitments: by **2030, Microsoft will be carbon negative** (meaning it will remove more CO₂ than it emits across Scope 1, 2, and 3), and by 2050 it plans to **remove all CO₂ it has ever emitted since founding**. As part of this, Microsoft set an explicit target to **reduce Scope 3 emissions by 55% by 2030**. Data center construction and hardware supply chain are significant chunks of Microsoft's Scope 3. Microsoft also established a \$1 billion **Climate Innovation Fund** to invest in climate solutions that help it achieve these goals.

**Relevant Initiatives:** Microsoft has been very active in the green materials space, directly funding and piloting new technologies. Through its Climate Innovation Fund, Microsoft has invested in **CarbonCure** and **Prometheus Materials** (low-carbon concrete innovators) and in **Boston Metal** (green steel). In a groundbreaking experiment, Microsoft is **building new data centers using cross-laminated timber (CLT)** instead of traditional concrete and steel - this hybrid design cut embodied carbon by 35-65% in pilot projects. Microsoft is a founding member of the **Sustainable Steel Buyers Platform** (with an internal lead for materials decarbonization) and publicly stated that enabling low-carbon steel is *"essential for meeting our carbon negative by 2030 goal"*. The company also reports testing concrete mixes that achieve over 50% carbon reduction versus conventional concrete. On the supply chain side, Microsoft is engaging its hardware manufacturers to use recycled and low-carbon components.

**Opportunity Alignment:** Microsoft's actions already illustrate the power of direct investment: its early funding and collaboration have helped scale companies like CarbonCure and CarbonBuilt, which Microsoft can then benefit from by using their concrete in projects. By further **investing upstream in green steel, cement, and other materials**, Microsoft can ensure that by the late 2020s, when its carbon-negative deadline approaches, it has dramatically lower Scope 3 emissions to neutralize. It essentially "buys down" the future emissions curve. For example, if Microsoft helps Boston Metal commercialize zero-carbon steel, that steel could be used in Azure data centers or even Xbox manufacturing, cutting Scope 3 without waiting for the general market to catch up. This accelerates Microsoft's progress vs a scenario of relying on offsets or hoping suppliers decarbonize in time. Moreover, Microsoft can leverage its **vast procurement power and capital** to achieve economies of scale for these nascent tech. Its data center construction spend (many billions per year) can be partly channeled as guaranteed demand for green materials, de-risking the investment to produce them. Such direct deployment of capital is likely to yield more **permanent CO₂ reductions** than purchasing an equivalent value of offsets or carbon removal credits, thus giving Microsoft more confidence in hitting its 2030/2050 targets. Finally, Microsoft's commitment to climate action is part of its brand - pioneering the use of low-carbon building materials strengthens its credibility and creates industry benchmarks that others often follow.

### Meta (Facebook)

The payoff is clear: every ton of CO₂ not emitted during construction is a ton Meta doesn't have to offset or remove to reach net-zero. ## Comprehensive Lecture Draft - *"HyperScale, HyperImpact: Investing Upstream for Climate Action"*

**Introduction (Scene-Setting & Problem Statement)**
Imagine a shiny new hyperscale data center rising from a construction site - a massive building filled with servers that power our digital lives. Now consider this: before a single kilowatt-hour ever flows to those servers, that data center has already emitted tens of thousands of tons of CO₂ **just from its construction**. How? Through the production of the **steel beams, concrete foundation, aluminum racks, miles of copper wire, and even the rare-earth magnets in its backup generators**. These *embodied emissions* - Scope 3 carbon that is emitted upstream in the supply chain - are the hidden footprint of our cloud infrastructure. Today, as companies like Google, Microsoft, Meta, and Apple lead the charge on renewable energy and operational efficiency, a growing share of their climate impact lurks in this upstream supply chain. In fact, steel and cement, the backbone of data center construction, together produce roughly **15% of global CO₂ emissions**. We stand at a pivotal moment: even as hyperscalers decarbonize electricity and cooling, the *relative* impact of materials is increasing. If we do nothing, by 2030 these embodied emissions could easily become the majority of a data center's carbon footprint.

The central question we face is **"How can hyperscale tech companies drive deep decarbonization beyond their own walls - reaching into the mines, furnaces, and factories that produce the materials they consume?"** This lecture will argue that the answer is: by **directly investing in upstream green technologies** that slash emissions in those hard-to-abate sectors. We will explore a bold strategy where hyperscalers use their capital and influence not only to purchase low-carbon materials, but to *create* them - catalyzing innovation in green steel, low-carbon cement, sustainable aluminum, cleaner copper refining, and rare-earth recycling. This is a strategy of proactive intervention, going beyond traditional procurement and advocacy. And it's one with precedent: a decade ago, tech giants began signing unprecedented renewable energy deals, effectively jumpstarting the utility-scale solar and wind industry. Today, they can be the "market makers" for clean commodities in the same way, bridging the gap between nascent tech and industrial-scale adoption.

**Historical Precedent: When Industry Stepped Up**
To understand why this strategy is not only necessary but achievable, let's look at how hyperscalers have already transformed one part of the climate puzzle - renewable energy. In the early 2010s, utilities and governments alone weren't building renewables fast enough to meet climate goals. Enter companies like Google, Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft, who began procuring large-scale renewable energy through power purchase agreements (PPAs). Skeptics questioned: *"Why are data companies meddling in the energy business?"* But the results were undeniable. By 2022, corporate buyers (led by these tech firms) had purchased a **record 37 GW of clean power globally** - more than the entire capacity of some countries. **Tech giants became the top buyers of clean energy in the world**, driving down costs and propelling wind and solar into the mainstream. This corporate demand-signal approach was investment by another name: long-term contracts that enabled developers to finance new projects. It was essentially hyperscalers **investing upstream in their Scope 2 decarbonization**, and it worked brilliantly - today many data centers run on 100% renewable power because those projects got built.

Now we need a similar story for **Scope 3 and materials**. The difference is, instead of buying electrons, hyperscalers would be investing in tons of steel, cement, and other commodities - or rather, the new technologies that produce those commodities with low or zero carbon. There is also historical precedent in other industries: think of how corporate fleet purchasing helped kickstart the electric vehicle market, or how companies collectively agreed to pay a premium for sustainable palm oil to reform that supply chain. When big customers band together and put real money on the table, suppliers listen and innovation scales.

One could argue there's even an earlier precedent: the **geopolitical mobilizations of industry in the 20th century** - for example, how the U.S. government and companies retooled steel and aluminum production rapidly during World War II. Today's battle is against climate change, and once again heavy industry needs retooling - but this time, hyperscale tech firms, with market caps bigger than many nations' GDPs, can be the driving force alongside governments. They have the financial firepower, the long planning horizons, and the self-interest (meeting their own climate pledges) to justify bold action.

**Geopolitical and Economic Context: Why Now, Why North America**
We cannot ignore the broader geopolitical and economic context in which this strategy would unfold. Decarbonizing heavy materials isn't just an environmental issue; it's fast becoming a strategic one. Consider **supply chain security**: the production of critical materials like rare-earth magnets is highly concentrated in one country (China produces \~90% of them). This has caused legitimate concerns in the U.S. and Europe about dependence and potential supply shocks. Building out domestic capacity for things like rare earth processing (as companies like MP Materials are doing) is seen as vital for economic and national security. It so happens that the cleanest processes (with strict environmental controls, renewable energy, recycling loops) are likely to be developed in North America and Europe as they reshore some of this production. Thus, **investing in clean technology for materials can achieve two goals at once: climate mitigation and supply resilience.** When Google or Apple supports rare-earth magnet recycling or a green aluminum plant in Canada, they are also diversifying their supply away from geopolitically risky sources, all while reducing pollution.

Furthermore, **policy trends** are creating both carrots and sticks that make upstream decarbonization timely. On the "carrot" side, the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) has earmarked large incentives for industrial decarbonization - for example, generous tax credits for green hydrogen (which can feed green steel) and funding for carbon capture and demonstration projects in cement and steel. This means that any corporate dollars invested in these areas can be leveraged or matched by public funds, stretching the impact. On the "stick" side, the EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) is coming into effect, which will penalize imports of high-carbon steel, cement, aluminum, etc. In a few years, if a data center company sources steel made in a high-emissions way, that could carry a financial penalty on the European market. Even if hyperscalers don't directly import steel, their construction vendors do - so there's regulatory pressure mounting to procure low-carbon materials. By investing now in local low-carbon production, hyperscalers can stay ahead of these regulations, potentially saving money in the long run and certainly avoiding the risk of non-compliance or public relations issues around "carbon footprints" of their new buildings.

Geopolitics also intersects with climate in the realm of **critical minerals for clean energy**. Hyperscalers buy a lot of renewable energy as part of their operations - e.g., wind farms for 24/7 clean power. Wind turbines need steel (for towers) and rare-earth magnets (for efficient generators). Solar farms need aluminum (for frames) and copper (for wiring). By investing in decarbonizing those very materials, tech companies are indirectly greening the supply chain of the renewable energy technologies they depend on, reinforcing the entire clean energy ecosystem. It's a systems approach: ensure that the inputs to clean energy and digital infrastructure are themselves clean. In doing so, they also align with government priorities - for instance, the U.S.-EU led **First Movers Coalition** explicitly enlists companies to create early markets for green steel, green shipping fuel, etc., to meet climate goals. Hyperscalers are uniquely positioned to be those "first movers" on materials.

In summary, the timing is ripe. We have supportive policy tailwinds, urgent climate headwinds (with scientists warning that heavy industry emissions must peak by 2030 to hit 1.5 °C), and a geopolitical rationale to reinvest in North American manufacturing might - but this time, building *clean* foundries and mills. The next part of the lecture will delve into specifics: what technologies and companies are leading this charge, and how investments might be structured.

**Technological Landscape: Sectors and Innovators to Watch**
Let's briefly survey the key sectors and the "green tech" each is birthing, as this underpins *where* hyperscalers could invest:

* **Green Steel:** Historically made with coal in blast furnaces, steel can now be made with **green hydrogen** or even direct electrolysis. In Sweden, startup H2 Green Steel and the HYBRIT project have already produced the world's first batches of virtually fossil-free steel, proving it can be done. In the U.S., companies like **Boston Metal** are piloting an electrolysis process that emits oxygen instead of CO₂. These need capital to scale from pilot to full-scale steel mills. Notably, steelmakers say they'll need an extra **\$6 billion per year** in investment to transition the global fleet of plants, plus trillions in new energy infrastructure. This is a gap too large for the steel industry alone - but not too large when spread across governments, banks, and yes, large corporate customers. Hyperscalers can co-invest in or pre-purchase from the first green steel plants. For example, Microsoft's participation in a collective purchase of **2 million tons of near-zero steel** by 2030 is essentially a form of investment - guaranteeing revenue for whoever builds those mills.

* **Low-Carbon Cement & Concrete:** Because cement's chemistry inherently releases CO₂, breakthrough approaches are needed (alternative chemistries, carbon capture, etc.). Several startups (CarbonCure, CarbonBuilt, Brimstone, Fortera) are developing ways to cut cement emissions 50-100%. But these need to be deployed in actual concrete plants. Meta's agreement to retrofit additional plants with CarbonBuilt's CO₂-curing tech is a prime example of a hyperscaler scaling up a climate solution. If Google, for instance, decided to spend (or finance) \$50 million to equip concrete suppliers near all its data center sites with carbon-cured concrete technology, it could dramatically reduce its embodied emissions. The return is not financial in the traditional sense, but in the form of guaranteed emissions reduction toward its net-zero goal. We should view that as an "investment" with climate ROI. Additionally, in cement there's the aspect of **carbon capture**: installing carbon capture units on cement kilns is expensive and currently uneconomic. Here, corporate climate funds (like Microsoft's) could provide concessional financing or offtake agreements for captured CO₂, helping get the first few projects built. The technologies exist; it's about pulling them into the market this decade.

* **Aluminum & Copper:** These are somewhat easier technically - they electrify. Aluminum smelters running on hydropower (like those in Canada or Norway) already offer much lower carbon footprints. The pièce de résistance is inert anodes (Elysis), which Apple supported. Hyperscalers could follow Apple's lead and invest in the *next* inert anode smelter or even just agree to pay a slight premium to source low-carbon aluminum for all their server casings. Considering Apple's experience: the *first-ever* batch of zero-carbon aluminum was small and likely costly, but Apple bought it anyway - now, by 2024, that tech will be commercial and costs will come down. It took a corporate push to cross that valley of death. For copper, a technology like **Jetti's catalytic leaching** can reduce the need for smelting (thus cutting emissions \~40%). If, say, Amazon (with its vast data center subsidiary AWS) invested in deploying Jetti's tech at a major copper mine, the payoff might be securing greener copper for tens of millions of data cables. Moreover, that mine would produce more copper from the same resource, easing potential shortages. It's a win-win: more copper for the electrifying world and less CO₂ per kg.

* **Rare Earth Magnets:** This is as much about environmental cleanup as climate. Rare earth mining in China has been linked to severe ecological damage. Recycling offers a route to meet demand without new mining - but currently <1% is recycled. Why? It's not due to technical impossibility; it's because collecting and processing scrap magnets hasn't been valued. If hyperscalers (and other big magnet users like automakers) funded a couple of magnet recycling plants and committed to buy X% recycled rare earth magnets, we could jump to, say, 20% recycling rate within a decade. That would slash energy use by \~90% for those magnets. The climate impact is a modest a few million tons CO₂ saved, but the supply chain impact is huge - it creates a closed loop and reduces reliance on a few producers. The cost for such an initiative might be relatively small (maybe tens of millions in capex), which is pocket change for companies spending billions on data center hardware each year.

We see across these examples a pattern: **the technologies to decarbonize heavy materials are emerging, but they need a big first customer or investor to reach scale and drive down costs.** Hyperscalers can play that role, just as they did for renewable electricity. The lecture would then transition to addressing the skepticism or concerns about this approach.

**Counterarguments & Rebuttals: Addressing the Doubts**
It's reasonable to ask: *Why should a tech company dive into investing in steel mills or cement plants? Isn't that far outside their core competency?* Let's tackle some common counterarguments one by one, because understanding these concerns actually illuminates why direct investment is both feasible and necessary:

* **"This is not our core business - shareholders won't like us funding industrial projects."**
  Rebuttal: True, Google or Meta are not in the business of making steel. But they are in the business of managing risk and achieving strategic objectives. Climate change and carbon regulations pose a material risk to these companies' future operations and costs. Investing, say, \$100 million in a green steel venture could secure a stable supply of low-carbon steel that shields the company from carbon prices or material shortages. It's akin to vertical integration for the sake of risk management. Moreover, many hyperscalers have established **corporate venture funds specifically for climate innovation** (Microsoft's \$1B fund, Amazon's Climate Pledge Fund, etc.), signaling to shareholders that some capital will be used in service of long-term sustainability goals. When framed correctly, such investments are seen not as divestment from core business, but as **insurance and innovation** to enable the core business to grow sustainably. There's also precedent in corporate history: companies often invest in their supply chain - Toyota, for example, invested in steel production improvements in Japan when it needed higher quality steel for cars. Hyperscalers can similarly justify these investments as ensuring the quality and sustainability of the infrastructure their core business relies on.

* **"What if these technologies fail or turn out too expensive? It could waste money."**
  Rebuttal: Venture-style investing always carries risk, but that risk can be mitigated by diversifying across several solutions (e.g. funding a portfolio of 5-6 companies in different sectors). Some will succeed, maybe some won't, but the overall chance that *none* of them pan out is low - especially because many are already demonstrated (the first green steel has been made, the first carbon-neutral cement blocks exist, etc.). As for expense, early projects will indeed have a "green premium." But hyperscalers are uniquely able to absorb or justify that premium. Their profit margins are huge compared to those of commodity industries; spending a bit more on materials for a data center is not prohibitive when the lifetime value of that data center (in terms of cloud services sold) is enormous. Also, through investment and scaling, costs will fall. For instance, the **cost premium for green steel** is expected to decline to <\$100 per ton with scale and cheaper green hydrogen, which would add maybe 1-2% to the cost of an entire building - likely acceptable. We should also note that doing nothing isn't free either: there's a cost to carbon emissions (whether internal carbon pricing, future taxes, or offset prices). If a company ends up paying \$200/ton for carbon removal in 2030 and its data center build emitted 50,000 tons, that's \$10 million per building in hidden future costs. Proactively investing that kind of money now to avoid the emissions in the first place can be more cost-effective. In essence, *pay now to not pay (for carbon) later*.

* **"Shouldn't they just pressure their existing suppliers rather than fund new ones?"**
  Rebuttal: Supplier engagement is important, and it's happening - many suppliers are being asked to use renewable energy or improve efficiency. However, for these heavy materials, **the supply side needs transformation, not just optimization**. Existing cement plants and steel mills can only reduce emissions so much without fundamentally new processes. Suppliers often respond that they will deliver low-carbon materials if available, but in many regions, they simply aren't available at scale yet. By helping create new supply (or retrofit existing facilities with new tech), hyperscalers essentially empower their suppliers to meet the asks. It's a form of extremely effective pressure: not just "we want you to do X," but "we're going to help make X possible and then expect you to buy in." Also, if a current supplier is resistant or too slow, supporting a disruptive entrant (like a startup with a new approach) fosters competition and can prod the whole industry forward. There's evidence this works: Apple's move with Elysis sent a signal to the entire aluminum industry, and now companies like Rusal and Chalco are exploring similar inert anode tech to not fall behind. So investing in new players doesn't alienate suppliers; it often motivates them to accelerate their own decarbonization plans. In some cases, hyperscalers can then partner existing suppliers with new tech providers - a collaborative outcome.

* **"Governments should handle this with policy; private companies shouldn't have to foot the bill for decarbonizing heavy industry."**
  Rebuttal: In an ideal world, yes, carbon pricing or government investment would drive all these changes. But we don't live in an ideal world - policy is moving, but not nearly fast enough to deploy the first large-scale green steel plants or cement CCUS by 2030 at the required pace. The scale of investment needed (nearly **\$30 trillion cumulatively for hard-to-abate sectors by 2050**) is so large that *every source of capital* - public and private - must be activated. Hyperscalers contribute by de-risking technologies with early capital and offtake commitments, making it easier for governments and banks to then pour in bigger funding for widespread deployment. Moreover, these tech companies have **agility and motivation** that bureaucratic processes often lack. They can cut a check to a promising startup in months, whereas a government grant might take years to materialize. Climate change won't wait for the slowest mover. There's also an element of moral leadership: these companies have spent the past decade profiting immensely from global markets and resources; contributing some of that back into global climate solutions bolsters their social license to operate. Many have publicly stated they *want* to lead on climate. This is their chance to do so in a way that complements government action rather than waiting for it. And frankly, governments are increasingly expecting the private sector to share the load - the UN's climate summits often feature pledges like the First Movers Coalition, where companies are voluntarily taking on commitments to support nascent tech. So hyperscalers stepping up is part of the broader emergent norm of corporate climate responsibility.

* **"They should focus on their own operations first - data centers are already huge energy hogs, fix that before heavy industry."**
  Rebuttal: Indeed, efficiency and clean power for data centers (Scope 2) have been a major focus and will continue to be. But the reality is that hyperscalers *are* getting their operations in order - many are already running 24/7 on renewables or will soon, and they invest heavily in efficiency and cooling innovation (AI-driven cooling, advanced chips to do more with less energy, etc.). The operational emissions per unit of computing are dropping. As this happens, the *relative importance of upstream emissions rises*. It's a classic case of squeezing a balloon - when one side (operational carbon) shrinks, the other side (supply chain carbon) bulges out by proportion. To truly reach net-zero or carbon-negative, they can't ignore that bulging side. So it's not either/or; it's a sequential expansion of scope. And these companies have resources to tackle multiple areas in parallel - Google can keep improving data center PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness) *and* simultaneously help decarbonize cement. There's also synergy: some operational improvements depend on better materials (e.g., more efficient cooling might use new materials, or advanced batteries for backup power rely on sustainable mining). By engaging upstream, they might also discover innovations that loop back to benefit operations.

Having addressed these concerns, the path forward looks not only reasonable but compelling. Yes, it requires broadening the corporate mindset beyond immediate business boundaries. But hyperscalers pride themselves on moonshot thinking - Google's X, Microsoft Research, etc., all tackle far-out problems. Climate change is the ultimate problem needing moonshots and scaled deployment. It's absolutely within the remit of these companies to innovate here; in fact, stakeholders increasingly expect it.

**Urgency and the Cost of Inaction**
We've talked about *how* this could be done; now let's talk about **why we must act with urgency**. The year is 2025. The global carbon budget for 1.5 °C is almost exhausted. Heavy industry emissions - from making steel, cement, and other materials - are still rising slowly, when they need to be *falling*. The **International Energy Agency's net-zero roadmap** calls for steel emissions to drop 25% by 2030 and for near-zero emission steel plants to start coming online before 2030. Similarly, for cement, the industry needs to peak by 2030 and then decline rapidly, with widespread carbon capture and alternative cements deployed by the 2030s. These timelines are incredibly tight - essentially one investment cycle away. Infrastructure like steel mills or cement kilns built now will likely still be operating in 2050. That means **choices made in the next 5-10 years lock in emissions for decades**. If hyperscalers (and others) choose to ignore upstream emissions now, by 2030 it will be too late to meaningfully change course for 2050, because the heavy industry assets will have been built without climate considerations and we'll be stuck trying to retrofit or prematurely retire them later (far more costly propositions).

In concrete terms, inaction means the following: the data center industry could boom in the next decade - say it doubles capacity to support AI, metaverse, and cloud growth - and with it, the consumption of high-carbon steel and cement also doubles. Each new facility built with conventional materials is **"baking in" emissions** that then must be counteracted. The aggregate Scope 3 emissions could surge, making those 2030 net-zero pledges unattainable without massive offsets. Offsets or carbon removals are not only expensive - projected to rise in cost as demand soars - but also limited. There's a real risk that around 2030, these companies find that high-quality carbon removal (like direct air capture) costs, say, \$500 per ton and is available in only small volumes, blowing up the cost of meeting their commitments. The **environmental consequence** is even starker: if heavy industries remain on a high emissions trajectory, we likely overshoot climate targets, leading to more severe global warming. For the companies, that translates into more extreme weather disrupting operations (think of data centers overheating in record heatwaves or supply chain disruptions from climate-fueled disasters). In short, *inaction imposes both planetary and business risk*.

Let's put a number to the environmental stakes: One analysis found that business-as-usual in cement and concrete would emit **98 Gt CO₂ by 2050**, but a net-zero pathway could cut that to \~30 Gt. That \~68 Gt difference is gigantic - roughly two years' worth of total global emissions today. For steel, a fast-action scenario avoiding delay could save **14 Gt of CO₂ by 2050** compared to a delayed scenario. These savings are the difference between a livable climate and one with perhaps an extra 0.2-0.3 °C of warming. Environmental scientists warn of tipping points - like ice sheet collapses or Amazon dieback - that get more likely as we accumulate emissions. Every gigaton we *don't* emit helps reduce those risks. So when hyperscalers accelerate decarbonization by even a few years, they are buying down cumulative emissions, which is incredibly valuable to humanity. This moral imperative aligns with their public ethos: Google's motto was "organize the world's information and make it useful" - well, organizing the world's industrial transition might be the usefulness our era demands. Microsoft talks about being carbon-negative as "making up for our climate debt." To do that, preventing emissions in the first place via upstream investment is far more efficient than trying to suck carbon out of the air later.

If hyperscalers fail to act and instead continue business-as-usual supply chain practices, they also risk a *public trust issue*. Stakeholders (employees, consumers, regulators) are increasingly savvy - they will call out companies whose "net-zero" claims rely too heavily on purchasing offsets instead of actual emissions reduction. There's reputational capital in being seen as truly cutting emissions within one's value chain. Conversely, being caught out in 2030 as having met a pledge only by buying millions of offsets while your supply chain still pollutes could invite backlash or policy intervention (the EU is even discussing rules on corporate climate claims that might penalize such behavior).

To summarize the urgency: The next few years (2025-2030) are the window for action to set heavy industry on a new course. Hyperscale data center firms, due to their scale and resources, have an outsized ability - and arguably responsibility - to act in this window. Inaction means likely missing climate targets and facing both higher costs and climate damages later. Action now means seizing a leadership role in a historic industrial transformation, with benefits that far outweigh the costs.

**Acceleration Potential: The Hyperscaler Catalyst**
Let's envision the positive scenario - what does the future look like if hyperscalers take this strategy to heart? The acceleration potential is tremendous. Instead of waiting until 2040 or 2045 for steel and cement to slowly decarbonize under government pressure and late adoption, we could see major progress by 2030:

* By 2028, the world's first commercial-scale **hydrogen-based steel plant in North America** could be operational, largely because a coalition of tech companies guaranteed its first five years of offtake. Without that, maybe it would have been built in 2035 after others proved it out elsewhere. That time acceleration means millions of tons of CO₂ saved. And North America retains a steelmaking leadership, producing "Google Steel" or "Microsoft Steel" (imagine the branding opportunities for climate-friendly materials!).

* The concrete used in every new data center by, say, 2026 could be 50% lower carbon than in 2020, because companies didn't just ask for it - they co-developed the mixes, funded the testing, and even helped write new building codes or specs to allow alternative cements. This diffuses to the general construction market. Suddenly, what started as a hyperscaler initiative becomes an industry norm: contractors across the U.S. start offering low-carbon concrete as standard because the big customers made it mainstream.

* Recycling loops start closing: one can imagine an **"Apple loop"** where old iPhones are mined for aluminum and rare earths to make new ones, powered by clean energy - Apple is moving there. Likewise, perhaps a **"Meta loop"** for data centers: servers that reach end-of-life are sent to a state-of-the-art facility (possibly one Meta invested in) that recovers steel, aluminum, copper, and rare-earth magnets, which then go back into new servers or building materials. This circularity, enabled by upfront investment in advanced recycling tech, could drastically reduce the need for virgin mining and associated emissions.

* Hyperscalers' involvement can also shorten the R\&D timeline through their tech expertise. These companies can contribute more than money - they have AI and engineering talent that, as Meta showed with concrete, can optimize industrial processes. Google's AI could conceivably help manage an electrolysis steel process for efficiency; Microsoft's data analytics could improve carbon capture operations. In partnering with industrial firms, they can lend their cutting-edge digital capabilities to accelerate process optimization, essentially *greasing the wheels of innovation*.

In current course without this strategy, we likely see a slower, more fragmented uptake of green materials - maybe a pilot green steel plant here, a test of low-carbon cement there, mostly in Europe first, trickling to North America later. With hyperscaler intervention, North America could leapfrog in these technologies, and the timeline compresses. We could hit crucial tipping points earlier: e.g., the "green premium" for steel and concrete could drop to near-zero by 2030 instead of 2040 because scale was achieved faster, meaning after 2030, market forces would naturally favor low-carbon options without needing ongoing subsidies or special efforts. That's acceleration in a nutshell: actively pushing the system to a state where it becomes self-sustaining sooner.

Perhaps most strikingly, **corporate capital can act much faster than policy or even a lot of institutional capital**. A tech company decision can be made in months and a project executed in a couple of years. Traditional heavy industry turnover is decades. If hyperscalers decide in 2025 to infuse, say, \$5 billion collectively into these efforts (a tiny fraction of their combined cash on hand), they could unlock on the order of \$50+ billion in follow-on private and public investment due to the signaling effect and risk reduction. This is a catalytic ratio that could compress a 20-year transition into 10 years.

The multiplier effect can't be overstated. For example, once Microsoft demonstrated with its pilot timber-and-concrete data center that embodied carbon can be cut 50%+, it paves the way for all data center builders to replicate that model. Similarly, if Google's steel procurement coalition helps build a green steel mill, automakers, construction firms, and appliance manufacturers might all start buying from it, filling its order book. Each corporate action creates a market precedent that others can join, thereby *multiplying the impact beyond the initial investment*.

**Conclusion & Next Steps: From Vision to Reality**
As we wrap up, let's cement the key message: *Hyperscale data center companies have both the opportunity and responsibility to invest in upstream decarbonization of materials, and doing so will yield dividends for the climate, their own sustainability goals, and the resilience of their supply chains.* This is not a call for charity or distraction - it's a strategic extension of their climate leadership into the industrial domain, where it's desperately needed.

The next steps to realize this vision could include:

1. **Forming a Collaborative Investment Coalition:** Much like the First Movers Coalition, hyperscalers could pool resources to create a dedicated fund or initiative targeting green infrastructure. Imagine a "**Sustainable Data Center Materials Fund**" where Google, Microsoft, Meta, Apple (and possibly Amazon, IBM, etc.) each contribute capital. This fund invests in 8-10 promising ventures: a couple in green steel (for structural beams, server racks), a couple in low-carbon concrete (for construction), one in carbon-free aluminum (for equipment), one in advanced copper recycling, one in rare-earth magnet recycling, and so on. By working together, they share risk and align on standards (so one company's pilot success becomes available to all). This also sends a unified message to the market that the tech sector is *serious* about decarbonizing their supply chain.

2. **Scaling Demand Commitments (Offtake Agreements):** Words need to be backed by purchase commitments. Each hyperscaler can audit its material use and publicly commit that, for example, *"By 2030, 50% of the steel we use will be low-carbon steel."* Or "we will purchase X tons of carbon-neutral aluminum, even at a premium, to support that market." These commitments should ideally be binding enough to help a producer secure financing for a new plant. It's essentially replicating the renewable PPA strategy: guarantee demand, enable financing. The Sustainable Steel Buyers Platform's goal of 2 million tons of near-zero steel is a great start - hyperscalers could do similar for cement (e.g., collectively agree to buy a certain volume of carbon-neutral cement once it's available). The visibility of demand lowers the perceived risk for innovators and investors in those sectors, accelerating project development.

3. **Integrating Requirements into Procurement and Design:** Internally, each company should bake low-carbon material requirements into its **RFPs (Requests for Proposal)** for new construction and hardware procurement. For instance, when commissioning a new data center build, specify that bidders must include a plan to use low-carbon concrete and steel, or that a certain embodied carbon threshold must be met. This will force suppliers to seek out the innovations (some of which the hyperscalers might be invested in). It creates a pull-through effect. Simultaneously, design teams can be tasked with exploring alternative structural designs (like Microsoft did with timber, or perhaps using *less* material overall through smarter engineering). If such requirements are known 2-3 years in advance, suppliers can't claim surprise - they will have to adapt or lose business.

4. **Advocating Policy and Co-Funding:** Even as they act privately, hyperscalers should continue to push for public policy that supports these changes - it will amplify their impact. They can lobby for things like stricter building codes on embodied carbon (leveling the playing field so all builders must use cleaner materials), higher R\&D funding for industrial decarbonization, or green public procurement (governments buying low-carbon materials). They can also partner in public-private projects - for example, joining a Department of Energy pilot as an industry partner providing some matching funds and a test site. Their involvement can accelerate bureaucratic processes by bringing in an agile culture and additional resources.

5. **Transparency and Measurement:** To ensure credibility and track progress, hyperscalers should measure and publicly report the embodied carbon of their new projects and products. If they invest in these upstream solutions, their Scope 3 emissions accounting should start reflecting improvements (e.g., "embodied carbon per MW of data center capacity decreased by 30% from 2025 to 2030"). Reporting this creates accountability and also showcases the benefits of their investments to stakeholders. It basically closes the loop: we invested \$X in green tech, and here we see Y tons of CO₂ avoided in our supply chain as a result (citing life-cycle analysis data). This kind of quantification will help build the case to continue or expand such investments, especially in dialogues with investors or boards.

In delivering this lecture, I would end on a high note: the *vision of the future we can achieve*. Picture in 2035 driving past a gleaming data center campus. Its solar panels glitter on the roof, but also note the building itself - built with **green steel and low-carbon concrete**, perhaps with walls of engineered wood. The air is clean, no diesel backup generators (they've been replaced by battery and fuel cell systems). You might see a recycling facility next door where old server components are reborn. This data center is not a hidden emitter but a showcase of sustainability. And it happened because the companies behind it decided, back in the 2020s, to invest in the hard stuff - literally the hard stuff, like steel and cement. They chose to lead where they weren't expected to, and it paid off.

The environmental consequences of inaction - more heatwaves, fires, and floods - would have come for these companies and all of us. But through action, they have helped bend the arc of emissions downward in sectors far beyond their immediate control, demonstrating the ripple effect of corporate responsibility. It's a story of acceleration: accelerating innovation, policy, and market adoption to match the urgency of the climate crisis.

In essence, hyperscalers can become *hyperscalers of climate impact*, leveraging their scale for positive transformation. They've reshaped our digital world in a decade - now they can help reshape our physical industrial world in the next. History may well record that around 2025, a coalition of unlikely allies from Silicon Valley and beyond helped ignite a green industrial revolution, one that set us firmly on course for a sustainable 21st century.

Thank you. (And let's get to work making steel, cement, and electrons all green!)
