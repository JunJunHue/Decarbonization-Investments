Greening Tech Supply Chains: A Case for Bold
Upstream Decarbonization
Introduction: The Next Frontier in Climate Leadership
Major tech companies have set ambitious climate goals, but a critical gap remains: the heavy industrial
materials underlying their products and infrastructure. Steel in data center racks, cement in office
campuses, aluminum and copper in devices, and even rare earth minerals in electronics all carry a massive
carbon footprint. Global cement production alone contributes about 8% of the world’s CO₂ emissions, and
the iron & steel industry adds roughly another 7%. For many tech firms, upstream “Scope 3” emissions
(from suppliers and raw materials) account for the vast majority of their carbon footprint. Yet typical
strategies—like offsetting emissions, pressuring suppliers, or buying “green” credits—are incremental
and insufficient in the face of the climate crisis. The world must cut greenhouse emissions 43% by 2030 to
avert the worst outcomes, which demands transformative changes, not just efficiency tweaks.
This whitepaper argues that hyperscale tech companies (Google, Microsoft, Meta, Apple, Amazon, etc.)
should go beyond conventional Scope 3 management and invest upstream to decarbonize heavy
industrial supply chains at the source. Instead of treating clean materials as someone else’s problem, tech
giants can leverage their capital and influence to make steel, cement, aluminum, and other
commodities green from the outset, fundamentally restructuring supply chains to align with climate
goals. The sections below outline why this bold strategy is in tech’s strategic interest, provide historical
proof that long-term bets can pay off, and detail how proactive upstream investment yields greater benefits
than slow-paced, hands-off approaches. We also compare the risks of inaction versus the rewards of
moving first, and draw lessons from how tech firms already became market makers for renewable energy
—a precedent they can repeat for green commodities.

Why Tech Needs to Invest Upstream: Beyond Financial ROI
The case for upstream decarbonization isn’t only about direct returns on investment. It’s about strategic
risk management, resilience, and leadership. By committing capital to decarbonize the production of
materials their businesses rely on, hyperscalers can unlock multiple compelling benefits:
• Regulatory Insulation & Risk Reduction: Environmental regulations are tightening worldwide, and
companies that act now will be shielded from future shocks. For example, the EU’s new Carbon
Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) will impose costs on carbon-intensive imports like steel,
cement, and aluminum starting in 2026 1 . Firms that fail to cut embedded emissions will pay a
price, since they’ll need to purchase carbon certificates for dirty materials 2 . Proactive investment
in green steel or cement today means avoiding these compliance costs and securing continued
market access tomorrow. As PwC warns, with CBAM coming into effect, “the time to invest in your
supply chain is now” to gain a competitive edge and avoid fines or loss of market share. In short,
climate-proofing the supply chain is a form of insurance against carbon taxes, trade tariffs, and
supply disruptions in a decarbonizing world.

and talent. Customers and employees increasingly demand sustainability – a 2023 Deloitte

• Control Over Climate Goal Attainment: Investing upstream gives companies direct control over
emissions reductions that are otherwise outside their operational boundaries. Current Scope 3
approaches often rely on encouraging suppliers to act or buying offsets – measures that are indirect
and can be unreliable. Many suppliers, especially in industries like manufacturing or mining,
operate on thin margins and cannot afford green technologies without help. Simply pushing the
carbon problem onto them (or buying offsets of variable quality) is a shaky foundation for meeting
science-based targets. By contrast, if a hyperscaler helps finance a green steel plant or a lowcarbon cement facility, it ensures a supply of climate-aligned materials and locks in real emissions
reductions that directly further the company’s net-zero goals. This approach de-risks the path to
climate targets – rather than hoping suppliers will cut 50% of their emissions by 2030, the company
actively makes it happen. It’s a way of future-proofing the business, ensuring that achieving 2030
or 2040 climate commitments is within the company’s own span of control.
• Supply Chain Resilience & Security: As the climate and geopolitical landscape evolves, certain raw
materials face new supply risks. Fossil-based commodity supply chains can be volatile – e.g. steel
prices often swing with coal and electricity costs, and rare earth mining is concentrated in a few
regions with environmental and political constraints. Investing in greener production (such as steel
made with renewable energy or recycled materials, or recycling and novel mining for critical
minerals) can diversify and stabilize supply sources. For instance, a green steel facility using electric
arc furnaces and scrap metal not only cuts emissions but also reduces reliance on coal and long
global transport routes. Similarly, supporting sustainable mining or recycling of rare earths can
mitigate geopolitical dependencies. In essence, climate-aligned supply chains are more likely to
be robust supply chains in the long run, with less exposure to fossil fuel price swings,
environmental protests, or sudden regulatory bans. Early movers will have first pick of reliable green
suppliers, while laggards could face shortages or expensive procurement down the line.
• Innovation and First-Mover Advantage: By investing early, tech companies can help scale up
innovative low-carbon technologies and gain preferential access or pricing. Whether it’s
hydrogen-based steel, carbon-free aluminum smelting, low-clinker cement, or novel recycling
techniques, many of these solutions are in pilot stage and need large customers to reach
commercial scale. A hyperscaler that becomes a launch partner for such technology not only gets a
seat at the table to shape product specifications to its needs, but may also negotiate long-term
supply agreements at favorable rates. As green materials become mainstream, these companies will
have a cost advantage and secure supply, while competitors scramble to procure limited green
commodities. There’s also potential upside in developing new revenue streams – for example, a

proactive upstream strategy. And importantly, they have the financial muscle to do so – unlike many
suppliers, the hyperscalers can deploy capital at a scale that matches the $3.5 trillion per year
investment the global net-zero transition requires. It is a classic case of using today’s resources to
safeguard tomorrow’s business.

Historical Precedents: Big Bets that Transformed Industries
Skeptics might argue that investing heavily in decarbonizing commodities is too costly or outside a tech
firm’s core business. However, history shows that industry leaders often win by making bold, long-term
bets – even if those bets buck short-term financial thinking. Below we highlight several examples (from tech
and beyond) where major players invested in long-term system transformations despite short-term
costs, ultimately reaping transformative results:
• Tech’s Renewable Energy Push (2010s): A decade ago, wind and solar power were more expensive
and less developed. Yet companies like Google, Amazon, Microsoft, and Meta began signing longterm power purchase agreements (PPAs) for renewable energy, effectively paying a premium to
kickstart a market for clean power. The results have been astounding: Corporate clean energy
purchasing grew 100-fold in ten years, and even as demand surged, the cost of solar power fell
~71% and wind by 47%, thanks to economies of scale. By 2022, over 300 companies had contracted
77 GW of clean energy capacity – equivalent to powering 18 million U.S. homes – with tech firms
leading the pack. Amazon, Meta, and Google are now the top three corporate clean power buyers.
This early, voluntary investment not only gave the tech sector access to affordable renewable power
for their operations, but also transformed the entire electricity industry, helping make
renewables cost-competitive worldwide. The lesson: committed demand from big customers can
rapidly scale nascent solutions, driving down costs for all. Tech companies can replicate this
playbook for materials like green steel and cement, just as they did for wind and solar.
• IBM’s “Bet-the-Business” System/360 (1964): In one of the boldest investments in tech history,
IBM in the 1960s poured an unprecedented \$5 billion (over \$35 billion in today’s dollars) into
developing the System/360 mainframe computer family. This was at a time when that sum exceeded
IBM’s annual revenue, a true bet-the-company gamble. Many questioned the move, but the vision
was to create a long-term platform standard for computing. The gamble paid off spectacularly: by
1966, IBM was shipping 1,000 System/360 units per month at about \$3 million each, and the
architecture became the de facto standard in computing for decades. IBM’s dominance of the IT
industry for the rest of the 20th century was cemented by this decision to prioritize long-term
innovation over short-term profit. The takeaway is that transformative change (in IBM’s case,

ushering in the era of compatible computer systems) often requires massive up-front investment –
and those with the courage to make that investment can end up owning the future. In the context of
climate, tech firms must be willing to “bet big” on re-inventing industrial processes; the ones that do
could dominate their markets in a decarbonized economy.
• Amazon’s Infrastructure-first Growth Strategy (2000s): Amazon famously spent years barely
turning a profit – not because the business wasn’t successful, but because Jeff Bezos relentlessly
reinvested revenue into long-term infrastructure: warehouses, distribution networks, cloud data
centers, and more. In the late 1990s and early 2000s, Amazon focused on market share over
immediate profit, plowing earnings into building logistics capacity and new services. In 1999, for
example, Amazon’s sales hit a record \$1.6 billion, but the company spent all that and more
expanding its fulfillment centers and capabilities. This strategy “paid off because it helped
Amazon grow into a company that could handle millions of customers” in the years to come. By
sacrificing short-term earnings, Amazon established an almost unassailable delivery network and
also launched Amazon Web Services – both now pillars of its dominance. The short-term cost was
immense (Amazon incurred losses in many early years), but the long-term payoff was
transformative: today’s e-commerce and cloud computing landscapes are largely shaped by
Amazon’s early investments. The clear parallel for decarbonization is that short-term costs can lead
to long-term market leadership. Just as Amazon built the logistics backbone for modern retail by
spending ahead, today’s tech giants can build the green backbone for tomorrow’s manufacturing
and secure a stable, climate-aligned supply chain advantage that competitors will struggle to match.
• Ørsted’s Clean Energy Pivot (2009–2019): Transformative investment isn’t limited to tech – consider
Ørsted, the Danish energy company (formerly DONG Energy). In 2009, DONG was a profitable but
coal-heavy utility (85% of its power from fossil fuels). That year, management made a radical
decision: pivot to renewables, targeting 85% green energy and phasing out coal. Ørsted invested
aggressively in offshore wind farms and new green capacity, even as it meant writing off existing
assets and incurring short-term costs. The turnaround was faster and more successful than anyone
imagined. By 2019 – 21 years ahead of the initial schedule – Ørsted had achieved 86% renewable
generation and become the world’s largest offshore wind producer. This astounding transition
from coal to wind power not only proved financially viable (Ørsted’s market value soared as it
became a global renewables leader), but also helped transform the offshore wind industry from
niche to mainstream. Ørsted’s story shows that even in “old” industries, bold leadership and
upfront capital can reinvent a company and its sector. The takeaway for hyperscalers is that
supporting a shift to climate-aligned production (even outside their traditional domain) can yield
both tremendous environmental gains and business opportunities. As Ørsted did with wind energy,
so can tech consortia do with green steel furnaces, sustainable concrete, or circular material
systems – the result could be an industry turned on its head for the better.
Each of these examples was “transformative” in hindsight, yet required uncommon vision at the outset:
betting on unproven technologies or strategies, and weathering skepticism over the lack of immediate
returns. In the same vein, investing in decarbonizing heavy industry is a visionary play. It may not boost
next quarter’s earnings, but it can redefine markets and secure long-term success. Tech executives should
recognize that they’ve done this before (with renewable energy, with cloud computing, etc.) – and now is the
moment to do it again to tackle the climate impact of the physical infrastructure we all rely on.

Proactive Decarbonization vs. Incrementalism: The Strategic Case
Relying on conventional Scope 3 tactics – nudging suppliers, purchasing carbon offsets, or waiting for
cleaner options to appear – is fundamentally an incrementalist approach in a time that calls for
exponential change. Here’s why a proactive upstream decarbonization strategy beats incremental
supplier management or offsetting, both in effectiveness and in strategic value:
• Real Emissions Reduction vs. Paper Accounting: Offsets and renewable energy certificates can
only get companies so far, and their credibility is increasingly questioned. Many carbon offsets do
not truly reduce atmospheric CO₂ (e.g. if not additional or if they risk reversal), and in any case,
offsets are a zero-sum game – they shuffle emissions around rather than eliminate the source. By
contrast, investing to produce low-carbon steel or cement directly eliminates emissions at the
source. For example, financing a steel mill that uses green hydrogen instead of coal can cut 95% of
CO₂ per ton of steel, a reduction no bookkeeping maneuver can achieve. A recent industry report
noted that for buyers, few other Scope 3 measures offer the combination of such deep emissions
abatement, near-term availability, and tangible added value as buying truly green materials. In
short, proactive decarbonization tackles the problem head-on – your products literally contain less
carbon, rather than claiming neutrality through offset math. This is strategically superior because it
yields a supply chain that is objectively low-carbon, resilient to scrutiny, and aligned with global netzero pathways (rather than one dependent on yearly offset purchases of uncertain quality).
• Speed and Scale of Impact: The climate clock is ticking, and incremental approaches are often too
slow. Consider that if a tech company simply asks its myriad suppliers to each cut emissions 5–10%
through efficiency, the aggregate impact might be marginal – and hard to verify – over the next 5–10
years. In contrast, one big upstream project can move the needle significantly. For instance, a single
green steel plant (on the order of 1 million tons/year capacity) replacing a conventional plant can
avoid several million tons of CO₂ annually. Committing to buy (or invest in) such low-CO₂ steel at
scale could enable 20+ million tons of green steel production by 2030, given the right
conditions. Analysts emphasize that these volumes “will only be realized by 2030 if
investment...takes place in the next five years”, and that delays now would make corporate
climate targets “harder to reach”. In plain terms: proactive investment is the only way to achieve
large-scale decarbonization on the 2030 timeline. Scope 3 supplier engagement, while necessary,
is often too piecemeal and slow to aggregate into the needed cuts. Tech firms are used to thinking in
terms of scalable platforms – here, the platform is a decarbonized materials ecosystem, which can only
be built by front-loading support and capital.
• Overcoming the “Green Premium” Barrier: One reason suppliers haven’t already decarbonized is
the infamous “green premium” – cleaner processes can cost more (for now). Left to market forces, a
steel maker has little incentive to build a plant that produces steel at, say, 20–40% higher cost. But
this is precisely where hyperscalers can flex their muscle: by absorbing or helping pay that initial
premium to unlock scale and learning-curve cost declines. History shows that today’s premium is
tomorrow’s discount if we invest in scaling technology. Solar panels and batteries were once far
costlier than incumbents; now they’re often cheaper, thanks to early adopters and production scaleup. Green industrial tech is poised for the same trajectory. Currently, green steel costs on average
~40% more than conventional steel, but BloombergNEF projects that by 2050 it could actually
cost 5% less than fossil-based steel as new processes mature. In other words, the green premium
can flip to a green dividend – but only if we invest massively in deployment to bring costs

down. By partnering with steel, cement, or materials producers on first-of-a-kind projects, tech
companies can effectively buy down the learning curve. This is superior to waiting a decade hoping
prices drop on their own. Indeed, a coalition of buyers and producers (with policy support) could
slash the green premium much sooner. The Energy Transitions Commission estimates that initial
breakthrough steel projects will have a 15–40% cost premium per ton, which buyers will need to
help cover – yet those extra costs can be temporary. The strategic play is to treat the green
premium as an R&D investment in the supply chain, one that yields a competitive advantage when
costs inevitably fall. In contrast, an incremental approach avoids paying any premium now but risks
being stuck with higher costs or scarce supply later when regulations or markets demand lowcarbon materials. Proactivity lets companies shape the cost trajectory to their benefit.
• Market Shaping vs. Following: A proactive strategy allows tech firms to shape standards and
ecosystems in heavy industry rather than passively adapting later. If hyperscalers demand truly
green steel and cement (and perhaps even co-develop certifications or technology with producers),
they can set the bar for what “sustainable materials” means. This could drive industry-wide change –
similar to how big tech companies influenced suppliers to adopt electronics recycling or renewable
energy usage in the past. It also positions those companies as leaders sitting at the table with
policymakers and industrial CEOs to design the future of these sectors. The alternative is to follow
whatever slow path incumbents take, possibly ending up with patchwork solutions (like minimal
recycled content or offset-labeled products that don’t fundamentally cut emissions). Strategically,
becoming a market maker yields far more clout. It could even lead to new partnerships or
business extensions (imagine a tech firm co-owning a share of a green materials venture, blending
the lines between “tech” and “industry” – much as Tesla blurred “tech” and “auto” to its advantage). In
summary, proactive decarbonization is an offensive strategy to create the market conditions you
want, whereas incrementalism is a defensive strategy reacting to external pressures. Offense has the
potential to create competitive moats; defense often just keeps you in the game.
• Credibility with Stakeholders: As scrutiny of corporate climate claims intensifies, those who take
concrete action will earn trust over those relying on accounting maneuvers. Investors are
increasingly evaluating companies on climate risk and true decarbonization, not offset portfolios.
Governments too, via procurement or policy, favor companies that genuinely cut emissions. For
example, if public contracts or future ESG indexes demand proof of low embedded carbon, a
company that can show “our products are made with 90% carbon-free steel and cement” will
outshine one that says “we offset our supply chain emissions by planting trees.” Moreover,
employees and the public are savvy – they know the difference between reducing emissions and
outsourcing them. A proactive approach that delivers tangible outcomes (like partnering in the first
carbon-neutral concrete batch for your new offices, or the first rare-earth magnet made without
toxic processes) creates powerful storytelling and transparency. Strategically, this builds long-term
goodwill and loyalty among all stakeholders, acting as a buffer against criticism. In contrast,
incremental measures may lead to accusations of greenwashing or insufficient action, which can
damage reputation and invite activist or regulatory intervention.
In essence, proactive upstream decarbonization is the “Scope 4” strategy – acting beyond one’s own
footprint to drive systemic change. It acknowledges that a hyperscaler’s responsibility (and opportunity)
doesn’t end at the factory gate of a supplier; it extends to the very mines, mills, and smelters that shape our
world. By taking the initiative to clean up those foundational processes, tech companies can achieve deeper
emissions cuts faster, gain strategic advantages, and fulfill their climate commitments with confidence.

The Cost of Inaction: Comparing Risks and Rewards
Bold action does carry costs – financial investment, organizational effort, perhaps the need to develop new
expertise or partnerships. But inaction carries costs as well, often far greater and more irreversible.
Here we compare the long-term benefits of proactive upstream decarbonization with the risks of slow
movement or doing nothing:
Long-Term Benefits of Bold Upstream Investment:
- Secure, Climate-Aligned Supply Chains: Early investment in green commodity production ensures that a
company’s future raw materials are secure from carbon-related disruptions. This means fewer surprises
from carbon pricing, no scrambling to find compliant suppliers when new laws hit, and smooth operations
in a carbon-constrained world. The company can operate knowing its key inputs (steel, cement, etc.) will
remain available and affordable even as environmental regulations tighten, because it helped create the
compliant capacity. Essentially, proactive investment “climate-proofs” the supply chain – an invaluable
stability factor in the 2030s and 2040s.
• Competitive Advantage & Market Leadership: Firms that move first set the industry agenda. By
securing large volumes of green materials and possibly investing in IP or processes, a hyperscaler
can enjoy preferential access and pricing when these commodities are in high demand later. They’ll
also be the first to offer truly low-carbon products to a market that increasingly values sustainability.
This differentiation can justify premium pricing or greater market share. For example, if Company A’s
devices or cloud services are built on a carbon-neutral supply chain and Company B’s are not,
sustainability-minded customers (including enterprise clients with their own net-zero goals) will
gravitate toward A. Moreover, being a pioneer allows a company to build internal expertise in
sustainability tech, which can become a consultancy or service in itself (some firms are already
monetizing their renewable energy and carbon-management know-how). Overall, proactive
decarbonization positions a company as the leader in responsible tech, attracting business and
talent that follow that leadership.
• Long-Term Cost Savings & Efficiency: While initial projects require capital, over the long run clean
energy and processes can be cheaper and more efficient. Renewable electricity often costs less to
operate than fossil energy. Green hydrogen and recycled materials, once scaled, could reduce
exposure to volatile commodity prices (e.g., scrap-based electric-arc steel is not subject to coking
coal supply issues). Additionally, by integrating vertically or contracting directly for materials,
companies can cut out middlemen and possibly negotiate better rates. Consider Apple’s approach:
by investing in carbon-free aluminum smelting, it not only slashes emissions but may eventually
acquire aluminum at competitive cost without paying for carbon allowances or offsets. There’s also
the benefit of avoiding future carbon taxes or penalties – an investment now is cheaper than a
decade of paying for carbon-intensive inputs. In short, today’s expenditure can be seen as buying an
annuity of future savings under a carbon-priced economy. Furthermore, many decarbonization
measures have co-benefits like improved energy efficiency or reduced waste, which inherently lower
operating costs over time.

Costs and Risks of Inaction or Slow Movement:
- Regulatory and Market Penalties: As discussed, doing nothing means exposure to future carbon costs
and compliance burdens. The EU CBAM is a prime example – a company importing high-carbon materials
into Europe in a few years will directly pay a hefty levy tied to EU carbon prices 2 . Similar policies are
expected elsewhere as countries strive for climate goals. Inaction essentially mortgages the company’s
future with a carbon debt. Additionally, large customers (like automakers or governments) are starting to
decarbonize their supply chains; suppliers that can’t provide low-carbon products will simply lose business.
In the consumer space, products could face labeling or disclosure requirements (“embedded CO₂ per
device”) that make a high-carbon supply chain a competitive disadvantage. The risk of inaction is being
left with stranded assets and a supply chain that’s out of step with the market, incurring higher costs
and losing sales.
• Higher Long-Term Costs & Lost Savings: Paradoxically, trying to save money now by not
investing can lead to higher costs later. For instance, if a company delays switching to green steel
until it’s mandated, it might face a period of paying both for expensive offsets and for expensive lastminute procurement of the limited green steel available (because it did nothing to help scale supply).
Meanwhile, competitors who invested early will have scaled supply with lower costs. We saw this
dynamic in renewable energy: companies that signed long-term renewable contracts early locked in
good prices, whereas latecomers sometimes face higher PPA prices due to increased demand. The
same will likely happen with green commodities. Inaction also means missing out on the efficiency
improvements that often come with process innovation – for example, modern electric furnaces can
be more flexible and require less maintenance than old blast furnaces, etc. If you’re not part of that
innovation, you don’t benefit from any productivity gains it brings. Thus, inaction can actually
erode a company’s cost competitiveness over time.

In summary, the long-term benefits of bold action far outweigh the transitory costs, whereas the longterm costs of inaction could be existential. Executives should view upstream decarbonization investment as
a prudent long-term strategy: it is buying insurance against future disruption, purchasing a call option
on new markets, and investing in the brand and resilience of the company. Yes, it requires capital and
coordination, but hyperscalers are uniquely positioned to marshal both. The biggest risk would be to
underestimate how quickly the landscape is changing – whether in policy, economics, or stakeholder
expectations – and to find one’s company on the wrong side of history and market dynamics. Far better to
lead from the front, shape the future, and reap the rewards.

Tech Firms as Market Makers for Green Commodities: Lessons from
Renewables
One need not hypothesize about the impact of corporate demand on global markets – the renewable
energy revolution of the past decade provides a powerful proof of concept. Tech companies, in particular,
have already demonstrated how their procurement decisions can accelerate an entire sector’s transition.
The challenge now is to repeat that success for industrial commodities.
In the 2010s, as mentioned, hyperscalers became anchor buyers of renewable power. They signed longterm contracts for wind and solar farms, even investing in projects, to supply their data centers with clean
energy. This was driven by sustainability goals, but it had cascading effects. By 2022, corporations globally
had signed a record 36.7 GW of renewable PPAs just in that year, with the tech sector accounting for the
majority. Such firm demand gave developers the confidence (and financing) to build more projects, which in
turn scaled manufacturing for turbines and solar panels, driving costs down. The American Clean Power
Association noted a 100× increase in corporate clean power procurement over the last decade,
accompanied by dramatic cost declines for renewables. Essentially, corporate buyers acted as market
makers – creating a guaranteed market where one didn’t previously exist at scale, until economies of
scale kicked in. Companies like Google proudly achieved “100% renewable” operations not by waiting for the
grid to go green, but by catalyzing new green power capacity themselves.
The precedent is clear. Now, imagine applying the same approach to green steel, green cement, and
other materials: long-term offtake agreements, advance purchase commitments, even co-investments in
first-of-a-kind facilities. This is starting to happen. Initiatives like the First Movers Coalition, launched by
the World Economic Forum and U.S. government, unite over 100 companies (including several tech giants)
to pledge early purchases of low-carbon products in “hard-to-abate” sectors 5 6 . For example, members
of this coalition have committed to buy a certain percentage of steel and aluminum from near-zero carbon
sources by 2030, effectively sending a demand signal to the market. Already, we’ve seen deals
materialize: automakers and even tech firms have inked contracts for the first batches of fossil-free steel
(such as Volvo purchasing steel made with hydrogen, or Mercedes-Benz contracting 50,000 tons from a
green steel startup). In the U.S., a group of major steel buyers – including Microsoft – recently banded
together to request millions of tons of near-zero emissions steel, in a collective push to persuade
steelmakers to invest in clean capacity 7 . These moves echo the renewable playbook: aggregate demand,
guarantee a market, and thus de-risk the supplier’s investment in new technology.
Tech companies have a unique role to play as market makers because they often have the necessary
scale, creditworthiness, and long-term outlook. A steel mill or cement plant requires a huge upfront
investment and may operate for decades – its backers need confidence that there will be buyers for its
premium-priced green product in the early years. A 15-year purchase agreement from a Google or
Microsoft provides exactly that confidence (much as their 15–20 year PPAs did for wind farms). It effectively
says: “Build it, and we will buy.” This is arguably more important for industrial decarbonization than even for
power, because heavy industry has thinner margins and higher technology risk. As BloombergNEF
observes, in the near term private-sector demand will drive industrial decarbonization, one offtake
agreement at a time, while policy catches up, and that sectors that can absorb the green premium
(like high-margin tech or auto) must lead the way. In practice, that means hyperscalers committing to
purchase a portion of their construction steel, server aluminum, or building concrete from low-carbon

10

sources even if it costs more for now – exactly as they were willing to pay a bit extra for wind/solar in the
early days.
Another lesson from renewables is the importance of innovative financing. Tech firms didn’t necessarily
own and operate wind farms themselves; they often signed “contracts for difference” or creative PPAs that
guaranteed a price floor, enabling project developers to get bank loans. Similar creativity can be applied via
green material purchase contracts or co-investment models. For instance, a company might agree to
pay a fixed “green premium” per ton of steel for 10 years, giving the steelmaker a predictable return to
justify building a $1 billion hydrogen DRI plant. In some cases, buyers might take an equity stake or provide
cheap debt for such projects, blending roles of customer and financier. Apple’s approach to materials
provides a concrete example: Apple issued \$4.7 billion in green bonds specifically to fund low-carbon
manufacturing technologies in its supply chain 8 . Through this mechanism, Apple helped fund the
world’s first commercial-scale carbon-free aluminum smelter (Elysis) and then became the first buyer of
that ultra-low-carbon aluminum for its iPhone SE. Essentially, Apple acted as a market maker and catalyst
– providing capital to get a climate-friendly technology out of the lab and into industrial reality, and in
return securing a first-mover supply of a critical material with drastically reduced emissions. This mirrors its
earlier strategy of investing in renewable energy projects to power its operations.
The renewables experience also teaches that early action can influence policy. Corporate demand for clean
power demonstrated to regulators that more ambitious renewable targets were feasible, and it built
constituencies in many U.S. states and countries for cleaner grids. Likewise, if tech companies start
demanding green steel and buying it, governments are more likely to enact supportive measures (like
infrastructure spending on hydrogen or carbon capture, or stricter standards that favor those prepared).
Moreover, standard-setting often involves early movers – companies that have figured out how to track
and verify supply chain emissions can help create the accounting standards and certifications (for “green
steel” or “net-zero concrete”) that later become industry norms. Tech firms are comfortable in these
coalitions, as seen in their participation in groups like the Science Based Targets initiative or renewable
energy buyers alliances. Bringing that collaborative clout to heavy industry will amplify impact.
Finally, tech’s renewable push showed that public commitments drive internal innovation. When a
company commits to 100% renewable energy or to use X% recycled material, it galvanizes its teams to find
solutions. Google’s quest for 24/7 carbon-free electricity by 2030 is pushing it into advanced energy storage,
smart load management, and policy advocacy. Similarly, a bold commitment such as “by 2030, 50% of the
materials in our products (or buildings) will be from zero-carbon sources” would force cross-functional
creativity – sourcing teams, engineers, sustainability experts working together to hit the target. It sets a
clear, quantifiable goal that can rally the organization and produce technology spillovers (perhaps inventing
new low-carbon alloys, or AI to optimize material circularity, etc.). In contrast, a vague aim to “encourage
suppliers to reduce emissions” doesn’t inspire the same urgency or innovation. Thus, being a market
maker not only influences external markets but also transforms the company internally – turning
sustainability from a reporting exercise into a driver of R&D and operational excellence.
In summary, the renewable energy precedent gives cause for optimism: when the world’s largest
companies align purchasing power with climate ambition, seemingly impossible shifts can happen in just a
few years. Tech firms helped renewables cross the chasm; now they can do the same for green cement,
steel, aluminum, and more. The key lessons are to commit early, commit publicly, structure deals that
reduce supplier risk, and partner broadly (with peers, startups, governments) to achieve critical mass. By
being the first buyers and investors, hyperscalers can ensure these currently nascent solutions reach

11

commercial viability just in time to meet the world’s 2030 and 2050 climate deadlines. Just as importantly,
they will fulfill the promise made when they claimed climate leadership – extending their impact from their
own operations to the farthest reaches of their supply network, truly “greening” the backbone of the
digital economy.

Conclusion: Building the Climate-Aligned Supply Chain of the
Future
The climate crisis demands nothing less than a fundamental remaking of industrial systems. As this paper
has argued, major tech companies have both the incentive and the ability to drive that remaking by
investing in decarbonizing the heavy industries that feed into their products. This is a natural extension of
their climate leadership: having optimized their own operations’ energy use and emissions, the next horizon
is to tackle the embodied carbon in the steel, concrete, metals, and minerals that make modern
technology possible. It is a daunting challenge – these sectors are often seen as “hard to abate” – but it is
also an immense opportunity for innovation and leadership.
By moving beyond incremental improvements and instead thinking in terms of systems change, tech firms
can help create a future where the default inputs to our economy are climate-friendly. Imagine a world circa
2035 where “green steel” is simply steel, where every new building is made with low-carbon cement,
where every EV or smartphone contains only responsibly produced metals. That world will not emerge
on its own in time; it needs bold action from those with the power to shape markets. The hyperscalers, with
their multi-billion dollar supplier spends and culture of innovation, are ideally positioned to be the
catalysts. They can bring to heavy industry what they brought to cloud computing and AI: ambition,
investment, and acceleration.
To get practical, tech executives should consider steps such as: establishing dedicated climate investment
funds or vehicles for supply chain decarbonization (similar to Amazon’s Climate Pledge Fund, but focused
upstream); setting specific procurement targets for green materials (e.g. “X% of our structural steel
purchases will be near-zero CO₂ by 2030”); entering into consortiums to fund pilot plants (for novel cement
chemistries or direct reduced iron with hydrogen); advocating for and helping design smart policies (like
green public procurement standards or joint industry protocols for emissions data sharing); and continuing
to transparently report progress. Initial projects should be pursued in partnership with experienced
industrial players – the learning curve will be steep, but cross-industry collaboration is a feature, not a bug,
of this process.
Yes, there will be hurdles. Not every early project will succeed technically or financially – but that is all the
more reason for cash-rich tech firms to lead, as they can absorb failures and learn from them. Yes, it will
require venturing beyond one’s core competencies – but leading companies have done so time and again
when necessary (whether it was Apple investing in chip fabrication or Amazon building a logistics empire,
stepping out of comfort zones is what they do). Compared to the scale of these businesses and the capital
they routinely deploy for share buybacks or acquisitions, the investment needed to green their supply
chains is entirely within reach – especially when shared among multiple partners and balanced by the
significant long-term paybacks outlined.
The cost of complacency is far higher. If even the most resourceful companies in the world shy away from
addressing the foundational emissions of industry, we simply will not hit global climate targets. Tech

12

companies risk undermining their own climate pledges and facing a future where they must severely curtail
growth or scramble for last-ditch solutions under regulatory duress. Conversely, by acting now, they can
help ensure that economic growth and climate action go hand in hand. They can enjoy the peace of mind
that their astounding technological achievements are built on a sustainable foundation – that the
cloud is running on green electrons and built with green steel, that the next billion smartphones won’t
contribute to climate catastrophe in their making.
In conclusion, this is a call for bold upstream climate investment as a strategic imperative. Executives at
hyperscalers should view it not as philanthropy or compliance, but as visionary business strategy. It is
about shaping the context in which they will operate 10, 20, 50 years from now. It is about resilience,
reputation, and yes, doing the right thing for the planet that sustains all business. The heavy industries of
the 20th century are ripe for reinvention, and they need the disruptive thinking of 21st-century tech leaders.
By mobilizing their capital and creativity upstream, tech companies can secure their own futures and leave
an indelible mark on the fight against climate change. It’s time to take the moonshot mindset that solved so
many technological challenges and apply it to the building blocks of our world. The companies that do so
will not only help save the planet – they will also be the architects of the new, green industrial
revolution, and beneficiaries of the thriving markets that revolution creates.
